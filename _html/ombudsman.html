An Ombudsman is someone who cares and wants to help. If you have questions or complaints about the care you are receiving, please contact the Long-Term Care Ombudsman Council. Your call will be kept confidential.

What is the Long-Term Care Ombudsman Council?

The Long-Term Care Ombudsman Council is a group of concerned citizens whose goal is to improve the quality of life for people who live in long-term care facilities such as nursing homes, assisted living facilities, adult family care homes and long-term units in hospitals.

What is an Ombudsman?

An ombudsman is a specially trained and certified volunteer who has been appointed by the governor and given authority under Flordia law to investigate and resolve complaints made by, or on behalf of, long-term care facility residents.

What does an Ombudsman do?

The ombudsman's job is to protect the rights of residents and assure that residents receive treatment and quality care by:

  • Investigating and resolving complaints

  • Visiting each facility at least annually to evaluate conditions.

  • Ensuring that residents are receiving the legal, financial, social, rehabilitative and other services to which they are entitled.

  • Acting as a mediator between residents, family members and facility staff.

  • Educating recidents, families and facility staff about residents' rights.

  • Providing information to the public about long-term care facilities.

  • Assisting with the establishment of resident and family councils.

  • Representing residents' interests before state and federal government by working to change laws, regulations and policies that affect those who live in long-term care facilities.

What kinds of Complaints can be investigated?

Complaints may be about the facility, its employees, providers of long-term care services, public or private agencies, guardians, representative payees or other persons who are in a position to threaten or interfere with the rights, health, safety or welfare of the resident.

Who Can use the Ombudsman Council's Services?

  • Residents of long-term care facilities who are 60 years of age or older

  • Relatives and friends of residents

  • Administrators and employees of facilities who have resident-related concerns

  • Any person or group concerned about the rights and treatment of facility residents.

What are Residents' Rights?

When individuals enter long-term care facilities, they keep all their rights as citizens, plus gain special "RESIDENTS' RIGHTS" under federal and state law. Facilities must post a copy of these rights in an area easily accessible to the residents, and must provide a copy to each resident upon admission. If a copy of these rights is not available from the facility administrator, contact the local Ombudsman Council office.

How do I file a complaint?

You may file a complaint in writing, by phone, or in person. When you contact the office, details of your complaint will be obtained and the case will be assigned to a volunteer ombudsman for investigation. You or the resident will be contacted within five working days. After a thorough investigation, the ombudsman will take appropriate action to resolve the problem and you will be notified of the outcome.

Do I have to give my name?

No, however it is better if we are able to contact you for more information. The name of the resident, the person making the complaint, and all details of the complaint will be kept confidential unless the complainant or the resident gives us permission to disclose the information.
